BREAKING UP THE USA

What would America look like if we split it in half?

As we approach the 2020 US elections, it is becoming increasingly evident that there is a significant divide between the Republican and Democratic parties. Differences in policies on contentious issues such as immigration, taxation and health care have created a significant amount of tension amongst voters and the broader population.

But what if we were to let the two parties, live separately? What if we divided the US into 2 states based on their political affiliations? What would these new countries look like?

To find out, let’s break America up into 2 states. A Republican state and a Democratic state
based on who the 50 US states voted for in the most recent election.

It’s important to note however, that states constantly "flip" between parties across elections. "Swing states" such as Florida tend to flip between being Republican or Democrat across different elections, (as can be seen using the "select election" slider above) but for now let’s cut up the US into two states based on 2016 election results.

From the map above, we can also see that states differ in terms of their political leaning or how Republican/Democratic a state is (which can be seen by selecting the “margin of victory” option above). We will factor in the effect of how strong a states political leaning is into later sections of this analysis.

Lets start our discussion with an analysis on the geography and population of these 2 new states.

REGIONS & POPULATION

Use the map below as a reference for the locations of the various regions & subregions. Tooltips contain additional information. Filtering via the legend is also possible.

Sub Regions of the New states

While we aren't breaking up the 2 states by subregion, it's interesting to note that the entire South East Central and South West Central sub regions will be transferred to the new Republican state, while the remaining subregions will be split amongst the two parties.

The visualisation above also shows a clear relationship between geography and political affiliation as subregions tend to consistently vote in favour for the same party election after election.

For example, the "South East Central" and "South West Central" regions have been 100% Republican since 2000 while the "Pacific" and "North East" regions have been almost 100% Democratic over the past 6 elections . Only one region, the "South Atlantic", has flipped multiple times within the last 6 elections.

Populations of the New States

If we were to split up the states based on 2016 election results, The new Republican State would have a population of 182 million people compared to 140 million for the Democratic state.

However, hovering over the chart we can see that there were actually more people who voted for the Democrats in 2016 (a difference of about 2% or roughly 3million votes), which may be a byproduct of greater voter turnout in Democratic states during the 2016 election.

While this difference of over 40million people, seems quite large, it isn't the largest we have seen. If we would have broken up the states based on 2008 election results, the Democratic state would have had over 127million more residents than the Republican state.

INCOME & AGE

Income & Age of the New States

Immediately from the bubble plot, we notice that Republican states tend to have lower household earnings compared to their Democratic counterparts (filtering the data using the party selection makes this quite obvious).

In fact there seems to be 2 clusters on the plot, one for each party. Zooming into the plot allows us to separate the individual states where there may be some overlap in the overview.

With regards to age, we can see that Democratic states tend to be a lot older, with almost every state (apart from California and Colorado) having an average age of above 38 and a total of 6 states with an average age of above 40. Republican states on the other hand have far greater variability in terms of age, with 3 Republican states having average ages of below 36 and 4 states with an average age above 40.

Distribution of Household Incomes

Arguably, the biggest difference between the 2 states is the distribution of household income. On average, Republican states are $20,000 poorer than their Democratic counterparts. In fact, the entire Republican distribution of household income is below the average Democratic household income.

Additionally, there is far greater variability in the Democratic income distribution. Despite having ~10 fewer states than the Republicans, the range of the Democrat state's income distribution is about $10,000 higher than the Republican distribution which is a lot more tightly packed.

EDUCATION, OBESITY, MINIMUM WAGE, COST OF LIVING

In the following visualisations, various demographic qualities are plotted against the "strength of a states political affiliation". Points further to the right represent states that had a large Republican majority victory in the 2016 election and states further to the left had a large Democrat victory. The dashed line separates Democratic states and Republican stats. You can hover over the individual points to find out the exact margin of victory.

Higher Education

There is a strong correlation between a states political affiliation and higher education levels.

Republican states typically have a lower proportion of people with a bachelor's degree (about 10% lower than Democratic states on average). The district of Columbia has an extremely high proportion of bachelor degree holders and also had an overwhelming Democratic victory in 2016 (87% higher than the Republican party).

Minimum Wage

There is a moderate correlation between a states minimum wage and the strength of their political affiliation.

It's interesting to note that every state without a minimum wage is Republican, likely because many republicans value classical economic schools of thought. Of the states with a minimum wage at or above $10, 15 are Democratic and only 3 are Republican.

Obesity Levels

There is a moderately strong correlation between obesity levels and a states political affiliation. Republican states seem to have much higher obesity levels when compared to democratic states (about 5% on average). This may be related to the fact these states typically have lower income levels when compared to democratic states.

Yet again, the District of Columbia is an extreme outlier and it has a very low obesity rate of 17.7% and lies perfectly on the regression line as well.

Cost of Living

There is a moderately strong correlation between a states political affiliation and their cost of living.

Democrat states are typically far more expensive to live in compared to Republican states. This is likely related to the difference in minimum wage laws and differences in incomes.

Hawaii is an extreme outlier in this case with the cost of a $100 item being twice as expensive compared to the average state. This is likely due to the large tourism sector and very high cost of housing (as only a small proportion of the state is suitable for housing).
